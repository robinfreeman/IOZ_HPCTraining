: Zoological Society of London {.infobox .vcard}

The **Zoological Society of London** (**ZSL**) is a charity and
organization devoted to the worldwide [conservation of
animals](/wiki/Animal_conservation "Animal conservation"){.mw-redirect}
and their [habitats](/wiki/Habitat_conservation "Habitat conservation").
It was founded in
1826.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}1[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-1)^ Since
1828, it has maintained [London Zoo](/wiki/London_Zoo "London Zoo"), and
since 1931 [Whipsnade Zoo](/wiki/Whipsnade_Zoo "Whipsnade Zoo").

On 29 November 1822, the birthday of [John
Ray](/wiki/John_Ray "John Ray"), \"the father of modern zoology\", a
meeting held in the [Linnean
Society](/wiki/Linnean_Society "Linnean Society"){.mw-redirect} in Soho
Square led by Rev. [William
Kirby](/wiki/William_Kirby_(entomologist) "William Kirby (entomologist)"),
resolved to form a \"Zoological Club of the Linnean Society of
London\".^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}2[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-2)^
Between 1816 and 1826, discussions between Stamford Raffles, Humphry
Davy, Joseph Banks and others led to the idea that London should have an
establishment similar to the [Jardin des
Plantes](/wiki/Jardin_des_Plantes "Jardin des Plantes"){.mw-redirect} in
Paris. It would house a zoological collection \"which should interest
and amuse the
public.\"^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}3[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-scherren-3)^

The society was founded in April 1826 by [Sir Stamford
Raffles](/wiki/Stamford_Raffles "Stamford Raffles"), the [Marquess of
Lansdowne](/wiki/Henry_Petty-Fitzmaurice,_3rd_Marquess_of_Lansdowne "Henry Petty-Fitzmaurice, 3rd Marquess of Lansdowne"),
[Lord
Auckland](/wiki/George_Eden,_1st_Earl_of_Auckland "George Eden, 1st Earl of Auckland"),
[Sir Humphry Davy](/wiki/Humphry_Davy "Humphry Davy"), [Robert
Peel](/wiki/Robert_Peel "Robert Peel"), [Joseph
Sabine](/wiki/Joseph_Sabine "Joseph Sabine"), [Nicholas Aylward
Vigors](/wiki/Nicholas_Aylward_Vigors "Nicholas Aylward Vigors") along
with various other nobility, clergy, and
naturalists.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}3[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-scherren-3)[[\[]{.cite-bracket}4[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-4)^
Raffles was the first chairman and president, but died after only a few
months in office, in July 1826. He was succeeded by the Marquess of
Lansdowne who supervised the building of the first animal houses, a
parcel of land in [Regent\'s
Park](/wiki/Regent%27s_Park "Regent's Park") having already been
obtained from the Crown at the inaugural meeting. It received a royal
charter from [George
IV](/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom "George IV of the United Kingdom"){.mw-redirect}
on 27 March
1829.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}3[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-scherren-3)^

The purpose of the society was to create a collection of animals for
study at leisure, an associated museum and library. In April 1828, the
Zoological Gardens were opened to members. In 1831 [William
IV](/wiki/William_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom "William IV of the United Kingdom"){.mw-redirect}
presented the [Royal
Menagerie](/wiki/Royal_Menagerie,_Tower_of_London "Royal Menagerie, Tower of London"){.mw-redirect}
to the Zoological Society, and in 1847 the public was admitted to aid
funding, and Londoners soon christened the Zoological Gardens the
\"[Zoo](/wiki/Zoo "Zoo")\". [London Zoo](/wiki/London_Zoo "London Zoo")
soon had the most extensive collection of animals in the world.

*A History of the ZSL*, written by [Henry
Scherren](/wiki/Henry_Scherren "Henry Scherren") (FZS), was published in
1905.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}3[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-scherren-3)^
The History was criticised as inadequately researched by [Peter Chalmers
Mitchell](/wiki/Peter_Chalmers_Mitchell "Peter Chalmers Mitchell") in
1929; both histories were labelled inaccurate by John Bastin in
1970.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}5[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-5)^

As the twentieth century began, the need to maintain and research large
animals in a more natural environment became clear. Peter Chalmers
Mitchell (ZSL Secretary 1903--35) conceived the vision of a new park no
more than 70 miles (110 km) away from London and thus accessible to the
public, and at least 200 acres (0.81 km^2^) in extent. In 1926,
profiting from the agricultural depression, the ideal place was found:
Hall Farm, near [Whipsnade village](/wiki/Whipsnade "Whipsnade"), was
derelict, and held almost 600 acres (2.4 km^2^) on the [Chiltern
Hills](/wiki/Chiltern_Hills "Chiltern Hills"). ZSL bought the farm in
December 1926 for £13,480 12s 10d. In 1928 the first animals arrived at
the new [Whipsnade
Park](/wiki/Whipsnade_Park "Whipsnade Park"){.mw-redirect}---two
[Amherst
pheasants](/wiki/Lady_Amherst%27s_pheasant "Lady Amherst's pheasant"), a
[golden pheasant](/wiki/Golden_pheasant "Golden pheasant") and five [red
jungle fowl](/wiki/Red_jungle_fowl "Red jungle fowl"){.mw-redirect}.
Others soon followed, including [muntjac
deer](/wiki/Muntjac_deer "Muntjac deer"){.mw-redirect},
[llamas](/wiki/Llama "Llama"), [wombats](/wiki/Wombat "Wombat") and
[skunks](/wiki/Skunk "Skunk"). In 1931 Whipsnade Park was opened to the
public as the world\'s first open zoological park.

In 1960--61, [Lord
Zuckerman](/wiki/Lord_Zuckerman "Lord Zuckerman"){.mw-redirect}, then
Secretary of ZSL, raised funds from two medical foundations to found
laboratories as an [Institute of
Zoology](/wiki/Institute_of_Zoology "Institute of Zoology") where
scientists would be employed by ZSL and undertake research.

The Society is a [registered
charity](/wiki/Charitable_organization "Charitable organization") under
English law.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}6[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-6)^

The [Institute of
Zoology](/wiki/Institute_of_Zoology "Institute of Zoology") is the
scientific research division of the ZSL. It is a government-funded
research institute, which specialises in scientific issues relevant to
the conservation of species and their habitats. The Institute of Zoology
focuses its research on five areas: [evolutionary
biology](/wiki/Evolutionary_biology "Evolutionary biology"),
[genetics](/wiki/Genetics "Genetics"),
[ecology](/wiki/Ecology "Ecology"), [reproductive
biology](/wiki/Reproductive_biology "Reproductive biology") and wildlife
epidemiology. The Institute of Zoology was graded 4 in the 1997--2001 UK
Research Assessment Exercise, and publishes reports annually. From the
late 1980s the Institute of Zoology had been affiliated to the
[University of
London](/wiki/University_of_London "University of London"). In 2000 this
was replaced with a partnership with the University of Cambridge, but in
2011, the institute became affiliated with University College
London.^[[\[]{.cite-bracket}10[\]]{.cite-bracket}](#cite_note-10)^

## Zoos and publications {#Zoos_and_publications}

[[\[]{.mw-editsection-bracket}[edit](/w/index.php?title=Zoological_Society_of_London&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Zoos and publications")[\]]{.mw-editsection-bracket}]{.mw-editsection}
:::

ZSL runs [London Zoo](/wiki/London_Zoo "London Zoo"), [Whipsnade
Zoo](/wiki/Whipsnade_Zoo "Whipsnade Zoo") and had planned to open an
aquarium, [Biota!](/wiki/Biota! "Biota!"). The society published the
*[Zoological
Record](/wiki/Zoological_Record "Zoological Record"){.mw-redirect}*
(*ZR*) from 1864 to 1980, when the *ZR* was transferred to
[BIOSIS](/wiki/BIOSIS "BIOSIS"){.mw-redirect}. The Society has published
the *Proceedings of the Zoological Society of London*, now called the
*[Journal of Zoology](/wiki/Journal_of_Zoology "Journal of Zoology")*,
since 1830. Since 1998 it has also published *[Animal
Conservation](/w/index.php?title=Animal_Conservation_(journal)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Animal Conservation (journal) (page does not exist)"){.new}*.
Other publications include the *International Zoo Yearbook* and *[Remote
Sensing in Ecology and
Conservation](/wiki/Remote_Sensing_in_Ecology_and_Conservation "Remote Sensing in Ecology and Conservation")*.

## Council {#Council}

The council is the governing body of the ZSL. There are 15 council
members, led by the president and served by the secretary and treasurer.
Council members are the trustees of the society and serve for up to five
years at a
